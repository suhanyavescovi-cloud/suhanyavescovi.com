Leadership · 5 min read

The loneliness at the top is real. So is the solution.

June 2026 · By Suhanya Vescovi

One of the things nobody tells you before you step into a senior leadership role is how profoundly isolating it becomes.

Not because the people around you disappear — but because the conversations change. Everyone has an agenda. Everyone is watching. And the one thing you need most — a genuinely safe space to think out loud — becomes the hardest thing to find.

I've seen this from both sides.

As a CEO, I know what it feels like to carry decisions that can't be fully shared with the team, the board, or even close colleagues. The weight of that is real. And it doesn't get easier with experience — if anything, the stakes get higher and the pool of people you can be truly honest with gets smaller.

As an executive coach, I've sat with founders and senior leaders who are performing brilliantly on the outside and quietly running on empty on the inside. High-functioning. Well-regarded. And deeply alone in the parts of the role that matter most.

Why leadership is structurally isolating

The isolation isn't a personal failing. It's a structural reality of leading at a senior level.

When you're the CEO or a founder, the people around you are — by definition — not in the same position as you. Your team looks to you for direction and steadiness. Your board evaluates you. Your peers are often competitors. Your investors have their own interests. And your friends and family, however well-meaning, can't always follow you into the complexity of what you're navigating.

So where do you go when you need to think something through before you have to perform certainty in a room full of people?

For many leaders, the honest answer is: nowhere. You absorb it. You manage it alone. You make the call and move on.

The cost of that isn't always visible. But it accumulates — in the quality of decisions made under pressure, in the energy it takes to keep performing, and in the gradual erosion of the clarity that made you effective in the first place.

The conversations that don't happen

In my work with senior leaders, I've noticed that the most important conversations are often the ones that never happen.

Not because the leader lacks insight. Most of the people I work with are highly self-aware. But self-awareness without a safe container to explore it in has a limit. You can only think so clearly inside your own head.

What changes things is having one person — just one — who is genuinely in your corner. Not tied to internal politics. Not managing their own agenda. Not performing loyalty or trying to be helpful in a way that's actually managing you. Just focused entirely on helping you think more clearly.

That kind of relationship is rarer than it should be. And for leaders who find it, the difference is significant — not just in performance, but in how they experience the role itself.

What actually helps

I want to be specific here, because "get a coach" is advice that gets thrown around loosely.

What helps isn't someone who validates everything you say, or guides you through a framework, or tells you what you want to hear. What helps is someone who can hold the complexity of your situation without flinching — who can ask the question that makes you go quiet, and then sit with the silence long enough for something real to surface.

Someone who understands business well enough to engage with the actual decisions you're facing, not just the leadership dynamics around them. Someone who has, ideally, sat somewhere close to the chair you're sitting in.

And critically — someone who has no stake in the outcome other than your clarity.

A question worth sitting with

If you're leading at a senior level right now, I'd invite you to ask yourself honestly: who in your world can you be completely unfiltered with?

Not carefully honest. Not professionally vulnerable. Completely unfiltered — about the decision you're uncertain about, the relationship that's costing you, the doubt you're not ready to say out loud in a room full of people who are counting on your certainty.

If the answer comes quickly and clearly, you have something genuinely valuable. Protect it.

If you paused — that pause is worth paying attention to.

Transition

The First 90 Days

Michael Watkins

Key Insights

  • You start at zero — even if you're exceptional. Reputation doesn't transfer. Every new role requires you to rebuild credibility from scratch, in a new context, with new stakeholders who are watching closely and forming opinions fast. The leaders who struggle most in transitions are often the ones who were most successful before.
  • Diagnose before you prescribe. The temptation to show value quickly by fixing things is real — and it's a trap. The leaders who navigate transitions best spend the first 30 days in genuine inquiry mode. Ask more questions than feels comfortable. The organisation will tell you what it needs if you're listening.
  • Read the situation with the STARS framework. Watkins identifies five distinct business contexts: Start-up, Turnaround, Accelerated growth, Realignment, Sustaining success. The leadership approach that made you successful in one will undermine you in another. The most common mistake I see is leaders stepping into a realignment and treating it like a sustaining success — challenging things that feel like they're working, without realising that's exactly what the role requires.
  • Early wins are about credibility, not results. Choose one or two visible, achievable wins in the first 60 days — not because they're the most important problems, but because they demonstrate that you listen, you execute, and you can be trusted with bigger things.
  • Assess your team faster than feels fair. You probably inherited a team that wasn't built for what you need to do. One of the hardest decisions in the first 90 days is working out who stays, who moves, and who needs to go. Most leaders wait too long out of fairness or uncertainty, and it costs them. The question isn't are they good people — it's are they the right people for where we're going.
  • Your relationship with your boss is a strategic asset. Most senior leaders are comfortable managing down. Managing up is the skill that gets neglected. Have the explicit conversation about expectations, success metrics, and communication preferences early — before assumptions harden into disappointments.
  • Build alliances before you need them. Map your influence landscape deliberately — not just your direct reports but the people whose support you need and whose resistance could derail you. The leaders who get blindsided at the six-month mark are usually the ones who invested too little in the relationships that sit outside their direct line of authority.
  • Know the five failure modes by name. Coming in with the answer before you understand the question. Isolating yourself and relying on your inner circle too early. Trying to do too much across too many priorities. Letting the urgent drive the agenda instead of the important. Neglecting to manage upward and outward while focusing only on the team below.

This is the book I come back to most often with clients stepping into new senior roles — not because it has all the answers, but because it asks the right questions at the right moment. What I've seen in my own work is that the frameworks Watkins offers are genuinely useful, but the harder conversation is usually about identity. Most of the leaders I sit with have built their career on a particular version of themselves — decisive, expert, delivery-focused. A new role, especially at a more senior level, often requires a different version. The first 90 days isn't just about strategy. It's about figuring out who you need to become, and being honest enough with yourself to make that shift.

Leadership

Good to Great

Jim Collins

Key Insights

  • Good is the enemy of great. This is the line that opens the book and it's worth sitting with. Most organisations never become great not because they fail, but because they settle. Good is comfortable. Good pays the bills. Good doesn't keep you up at night. But Collins argues that the comfort of good performance is actually one of the most insidious forces working against exceptional leadership. And honestly — I see this with individual leaders just as much as organisations. The ones who plateau are rarely the ones who are struggling. They're the ones who've stopped being uncomfortable.
  • Level 5 Leadership is about humility, not charisma. This one genuinely surprised Collins when the data came back. The leaders who took companies from good to great weren't the loudest, most visible, or most celebrated. They were quietly determined, fiercely ambitious for the organisation rather than for themselves, and consistently quick to credit others and absorb blame. It's a profile that challenges almost everything popular culture tells us a great leader looks like.
  • First who, then what. Before strategy. Before vision. Before direction. Get the right people on the bus. Collins found that great leaders don't figure out where they're going and then recruit people to execute the plan. They get the right people in place first, and then work out the best direction together. I come back to this constantly in my work — too many senior leaders inherit a team, work around the gaps, and wonder why execution is hard. The team question is always earlier than it feels.
  • Confront the brutal facts — without losing faith. Collins calls this the Stockdale Paradox. It's the ability to hold two things at once — an unwavering belief that you'll prevail in the end, and a completely honest reckoning with the reality you're facing right now. Leaders who protect their people from hard truths think they're being kind. What they're actually doing is eroding trust and delaying the work.
  • The Hedgehog Concept — clarity over cleverness. Great organisations aren't trying to be great at everything. They find the intersection of three things: what they're deeply passionate about, what they can be the best in the world at, and what drives their economic engine. I use this framework a lot in coaching, particularly with leaders who are ambitious but scattered. It's not about doing less — it's about knowing what you're actually for.
  • A culture of discipline — not a disciplinarian. Collins is clear on this: great companies don't need more rules or tighter management. They need people who are disciplined by nature, operating inside a clear framework of values and priorities. The leader's job is to build that culture — not to enforce it from the top but to make it just the way things are done here.
  • The Flywheel — momentum compounds quietly. There is no single defining moment in a good-to-great transformation. It's consistent pressure, applied in a consistent direction, over a long period of time. The flywheel turns slowly at first, then faster, then almost unstoppably. From the outside it looks like an overnight success. From the inside it felt like relentless, unglamorous work. This is probably the most honest thing in the book.

What stays with me most from Collins isn't the strategy frameworks — it's that portrait of the Level 5 leader. We live in a world that rewards visibility, self-promotion and bold declarations, and yet the leaders who actually build something lasting tend to be the ones most focused on the work and least focused on being seen doing it. I talk about this a lot with senior clients who are navigating the tension between building their own profile and building their team's capability — because both matter, and getting the balance wrong in either direction has a cost. The leaders who figure this out are the ones who build organisations that outlast them. And when you ask them what they actually want — that's usually exactly it.

The New Leader's 100-Day Action Plan

George Bradt, Jayme Check & Jorge Deluca

Key Insights

  • Start before day one. This is the thing most leaders miss. Bradt argues that your transition begins the moment you accept the role — not when you walk in the door. The leaders who hit the ground running aren't the ones who worked hardest in the first week. They're the ones who did the thinking before it. Who are the key stakeholders? What's the informal power structure? What do people actually think about the state of the business? You can find out most of this before you start if you're willing to ask.
  • Get clear on the burning imperative. Before anything else, your team needs to know why they exist, where they're going, and what the most important thing is right now. Bradt calls this the burning imperative — a single, galvanising answer to the question what are we here to do? In my experience, most teams don't have one. They have a strategy document and a set of KPIs. That's not the same thing. A burning imperative is what gets people out of bed when things are hard.
  • Manage the message before someone else does. In the absence of communication, people make things up. And what they make up is almost always worse than the reality. The first 100 days is a period of intense scrutiny — your team, your peers, your board are all forming views about who you are and what you stand for. Bradt is explicit: you need to be deliberate about the narrative you're creating, because one is being created whether you're deliberate about it or not.
  • Assess your team with fresh eyes — not inherited assumptions. You will walk into a room full of other people's opinions about your team. Who's good. Who's difficult. Who to trust. Treat all of it as hypothesis, not fact. The leaders who get this right spend the first 30 days forming their own views — through direct observation, honest conversation, and paying attention to what people do, not just what they say.
  • The 100-day plan is a tool for alignment, not just action. This is the part of the book I find most useful in practice. The plan isn't primarily about what you're going to do. It's about creating shared understanding with your boss, your team, and your key stakeholders about what success looks like and how you're going to get there. Done well, it surfaces misalignment early — when it's still easy to address.
  • Culture will either be your accelerator or your handbrake. Bradt is unambiguous about this: you cannot execute a strategy through a culture that isn't built for it. Understanding the culture you've stepped into — not the values on the wall but the actual lived behaviours and unwritten rules — is one of the most important diagnostic tasks in the first 30 days.

The 100-Day Milestone Map

This is where the book earns its title. Bradt breaks the first 100 days into four distinct phases — each with a different focus, a different set of priorities, and a different version of what good leadership looks like.

  • Days 1–30 — Listen before you leadResist the urge to fix anything. Your job right now is to observe, ask questions, and form your own views about the team, the culture, and the real state of the business — independent of what you've been told. Every organisation has a gap between its official story and its actual story. Your job in this phase is to find it.
  • Days 31–45 — Consolidate what you've learnedYou now have enough information to start forming a point of view. This is the moment to align explicitly with your boss on priorities — not assumed alignment, actual alignment. Start identifying your early wins — the visible, achievable moves that build credibility and signal who you are as a leader.
  • Days 46–60 — Build and align your teamThis is where the hard calls get made. If you've been watching carefully for the last 45 days, you know who's in the right seat and who isn't. Make the decisions — kindly, clearly, and sooner than feels comfortable. Establish your operating rhythm.
  • Days 61–100 — Set the agendaShift from learning mode to leading mode. Consolidate your early wins and communicate what they mean. Articulate the burning imperative clearly and repeatedly — more times than feels necessary, because in large organisations, messages travel slowly and land inconsistently.

What I love about this book is that it treats the transition as a project — which sounds obvious, but most leaders don't actually approach it that way. They show up, they work hard, they respond to what comes at them. Bradt asks you to be far more intentional than that. The thing I find myself coming back to most with clients is the idea of starting before day one. The leaders who do the thinking early — who get curious before they get busy — consistently have a better first year. Not because they have all the answers, but because they've already asked themselves the hard questions. And in my experience, the quality of your questions in the first 90 days determines the quality of your leadership for the next three years.

Leadership

Hit Refresh

Satya Nadella

Key Insights

  • Culture eats strategy — and Nadella knew it. When Nadella took over Microsoft in 2014, the company wasn't short of smart people or good technology. What it had was a culture that had turned inward, competitive in the wrong ways, and deeply risk-averse. His first and most important act as CEO wasn't a strategic pivot. It was a cultural one. In my experience, this is the move most incoming leaders underestimate — the strategy is usually the easier part.
  • Fixed mindset is a leadership crisis waiting to happen. Nadella built his entire cultural transformation around Carol Dweck's concept of the growth mindset — the belief that capability is developed, not fixed. He applied it not just to individuals but to the entire organisation. Microsoft had become a company of people who needed to be the smartest in the room. Nadella wanted a company of people who were most curious in the room. That's a profound shift, and it starts at the top.
  • Empathy is a business skill, not a soft one. This is the part of the book that catches people off guard. Nadella talks about empathy — openly, seriously, and repeatedly — as one of the most important capabilities a leader can develop. Not empathy as niceness, but empathy as the ability to genuinely understand what someone else needs. I've found this reframe genuinely useful with senior leaders who dismiss the relational side of leadership as secondary.
  • Clarity of purpose changes what's possible. Nadella reframed Microsoft's mission to empowering every person and every organisation on the planet to achieve more. That shift sounds simple. What it actually did was give every person in a 150,000-person organisation a new lens for every decision they made. Purpose at that level isn't a values statement. It's an operating system.
  • Partnerships over ego. One of the most striking things Nadella did early was form partnerships with companies Microsoft had previously treated as enemies — including Salesforce and Linux. The lesson isn't about partnerships specifically — it's about the willingness to let go of institutional ego when the business requires it. I see leaders struggle with this constantly, particularly when the ego is wrapped in a past success.
  • The cloud bet was a values decision as much as a strategic one. Nadella's decision to go all-in on Azure required him to cannibalise Microsoft's most profitable existing businesses to do it. That takes a particular kind of leadership courage. Not the loud, dramatic kind. The quiet, clear-eyed kind — the willingness to make a decision you know is right before everyone around you agrees.
  • Refresh doesn't mean reset. Nadella wasn't trying to tear Microsoft down and rebuild it. He was trying to reconnect it to what it was actually capable of — to find the energy and curiosity that had been buried under years of internal politics and defensive behaviour. The refresh was about recovering something, not inventing something new. That distinction matters enormously in how you lead a transformation.

What makes this book different from most leadership books is that it's written by someone doing the job in real time — not looking back from retirement with the benefit of hindsight. Nadella is candid about the uncertainty, the mistakes, and the moments where he genuinely didn't know what the right answer was. That honesty is rare, and I think it's what makes the book land. The thing I find myself talking about most with clients is his reframing of empathy — because in my experience, the leaders who resist that word are usually the ones who need it most. Not because they're unkind, but because they've built a version of leadership that keeps other people slightly at a distance. And that distance has a cost — in trust, in candour, in the quality of information that actually reaches them.
